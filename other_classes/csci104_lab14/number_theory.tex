\begin{prop}
    If \(p\) is prime and \(p \mid ab\), then \(p \mid a\) or \(p \mid b\).
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
    We know that \(\gcd(p, a)\) must be equal to \(1\) or \(p\). If \(\gcd(p, a) = 1\), then our helpful theorem gives \(p \mid b\). If \(\gcd(p, a) = p\), then we know that \(p \mid a\). Hence either \(p \mid a\) or \(p \mid b\), as desired.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}
    The first \(p\) probed locations are unique.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
    Assume for the sake of contradiction that the first \(p\) probed locations are not unique. Then there are \(0 \le i < j < p\) such that \(h + ik \equiv h + jk \mod p\). Algebraic manipulation gives \((j - i)k \equiv 0 \mod p\), hence \(p \mid (j - i)k\). From the previous exercise, this implies \(p \mid (j - i)\) or \(p \mid k\). But both \(j - i\) and \(k\) are strictly less than \(p\)! Thus we have contradiction.
\end{proof}
